The thesis develops and evaluates an obstacle-aware NMPC framework for the final berthing phase of a surface vessel operating at low speed in confined harbour environments. Final approach manoeuvres are characterised by tight spatial margins, frequent constraint activation, reduced manoeuvrability, and sensitivity to modelling errors and uncertainty in the motion of surrounding traffic. To address these challenges, the proposed controller solves a finite-horizon nonlinear optimal control problem online at each sampling instant, using a 3-DOF vessel model (surge–sway–yaw) and explicit constraints for actuator limits and collision avoidance. Dynamic obstacles are handled via short-horizon online prediction based on simplified motion models (baseline constant-velocity with optional uncertainty inflation). A key focus is practical real-time operation: the work studies solver convergence and runtime variability, and assesses how warm-starting from a geometric planner (e.g., A*/Hybrid A*) affects feasibility, convergence, and docking performance. Safety enforcement is investigated through three alternative formulations—hard distance constraints, soft constraints with slack variables, and control-barrier-function (CBF) based constraints—compared in terms of safety, conservativeness, feasibility, and computation time. The method is evaluated in reproducible simulation scenarios including cluttered static environments, dynamic traffic encounters, narrow passages, uncertainty stress tests, and solver deadline stress tests, all simulated in a matlab transposition of the map of the Helsinki harbour. The thesis is conducted as part of a two-person investigation at ABB Corporate Research in Västerås, where an integrated NMPC approach is contrasted with a complementary planner–tracker architecture developed in parallel
